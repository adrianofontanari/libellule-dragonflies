## 5. Inspirations

The inspirations behind the poems in this collection are many - different perspectives that allow for multiple readings of their meaning.

### The Wounded Child

Represents the traumas of the past, the wounds of childhood, the painful experiences that the adult learns to recognise and heal. Emotional themes of vulnerability emerge here - a confrontation with a state that will be no more. One goes back, reliving those experiences through adult eyes: this is how the inner child is embraced. Saved.

### The Romantic Dimension

It is not only the romantic dimension that acts as a motor for personal growth; the search for Her becomes a metaphor for self-love: confronting one's inner world, rebirth, solitude, waiting - all relevant as a psychological lever for transformation and self-awareness. A Calvinian state: *"They met. He knew her and himself, something he had in truth never known. And he would be him, though he had always believed himself capable, able -"* (*The Baron in the Trees*, ch. XXI).

### The Lighthouse

Symbol of self-love, of home, of inner guidance. The lighthouse is light in the uncertainty of the raging sea, first landfall after the shipwreck. Read on a double register: salvation for parts of the self and salvation in relationships.

## 6. About the Author

Adriano Fontanari was born in Cavalese (TN) in December 1995. Already in middle school, his inclination toward poetry was strong and was noticed by his Italian teacher (also a professor at the Faculty of Letters in Bolzano), who began sharing Adriano's unpublished work with his students. With a background in economics and healthcare from prestigious European and American universities, Adriano works in financial and healthcare innovation, collaborating with investment funds and startups. After several years in Trento, he moved to Milan, where he returned with renewed intensity to his literary work. Among his influences are Eugenio Montale, Rupi Kaur and Peter Gizzi.

Dragonflies is a proposal, a possibility, an invitation to leap, to take flight. Because no one knows whether in life they will manage to fly, but it is worth trying - experiencing that sensation of lifting, of being uprooted from the ground, which is worth the entire journey and holds the meaning of our passage.

> "I believe that, even if it is a succession of 'mere words', poetry has a social function. It is an instrument of freedom. It accompanies those who, in the lines they read, relive their own fragments of life, those who cannot find the words but manage to recognise themselves. In this sense poetry is a tool for awareness and a stimulus toward change."  
> — Adriano Fontanari, *The Lighthouse Keeper*
